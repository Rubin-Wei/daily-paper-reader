## Abstract 

Large language models with web search are increasingly used in scientific publishing agents, yet they still produce BibTeX entries with pervasive field-level errors. Prior evaluations tested base models without search, which does not reflect current practice. We construct a benchmark of 931 papers across four scientific domains and three citation tiers—popular, low-citation, and recent post-cutoff—designed to disentangle parametric memory from search dependence, with version-aware ground truth that ac-counts for multiple citable versions of the same paper. Three search-enabled frontier models (GPT-5, Claude Sonnet-4.6, Gemini-3 Flash) generate Bib-TeX entries scored on nine fields and a six-way error taxonomy, producing approximately 23,000 field-level observations. Overall accuracy is 83.6%, but only 50.9% of entries are fully correct; accuracy drops 27.7 percentage points from popular to recent papers, revealing heavy reliance on para-metric memory even when search is available. Field-error co-occurrence analysis identifies two distinct failure modes: wholesale entry substitution (identity fields fail together) and isolated field error. We evaluate clibib , an open-source tool for deterministic BibTeX retrieval from the Zotero Trans-lation Server with CrossRef as a fallback, as a mitigation mechanism. In a two-stage integration where baseline entries are revised against authorita-tive records, accuracy rises by +8.0 pp to 91.5%, fully correct entries rise from 50.9% to 78.3%, and the regression rate is only 0.8%. An ablation comparing single-stage and two-stage integration shows that separating search from revision yields larger gains and lower regression (0.8% vs. 4.8%), demonstrating that integration architecture matters independently of model capability. We release the 931-paper benchmark, nine-field error taxonomy, and the clibib retrieval tool to support evaluation and mitiga-tion of citation hallucinations in LLM-based scientific writing. 

## 1 Introduction 

Bibliographic metadata is one of the few categories of LLM output that can be verified against authoritative external records. A DOI either resolves to the correct paper or it does not; an author list either matches the published byline or it does not. When models fabricate or corrupt BibTeX fields, the errors propagate into manuscripts, reference lists, and downstream citation analyses. Unlike free-text hallucination, where ground truth is often ambiguous, every field in a BibTeX entry has a deterministic correct value recorded in publisher databases. Prior work reports hallucination rates of 14–95% in LLM-generated citations. Walters & Wilder (2023) found 18–55% fabrication rates in GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 literature reviews; Chelli et al. (2024) reported DOI accuracy of only 16–20% in medical citation generation; Aljamaan et al. (2024) showed that retrieval-augmented tools substantially outperform standalone LLMs on a weighted accuracy score; and Xu et al. (2026) documented 14–95% hallucination rates across 13 models. These studies share a critical limitation: they evaluate base models

The tier structure tests this directly: if search tools fully compensated for knowledge gaps, accuracy would be uniform across tiers. We develop and evaluate clibib , an open-source Python tool that fetches authoritative BibTeX from the Zotero Translation Server via deterministic identifier resolution. Given a DOI, arXiv ID, or other persistent identifier, clibib returns the publisher-deposited record without model involvement. We compare two integration architectures—a single-stage tool loop and a two-stage pipeline that separates search from revision—and find that the latter yields substantially larger gains with lower regression, demonstrating that how a tool is integrated matters independently of model capability. ## 2 Related Work 

Citation hallucination—where LLMs fabricate references or corrupt bibliographic metadata— has been studied along three axes: measuring fabrication rates, analyzing field-level accu-racy, and developing mitigation tools. We give a brief overview here; Appendix A provides a comprehensive survey. LLMs fabricate plausible but fictitious references at rates ranging from 18% to 95% depend-ing on the model and elicitation method (Alkaissi & McFarlane, 2023; Walters & Wilder, 2023; Xu et al., 2026). Among real citations, title is consistently the most accurate field while DOI is the worst; numerical metadata (volume, pages) are error-prone across all models (Chelli et al., 2024; Szeider, 2026). Niimi (2025) found a log-linear correlation between citation count and accuracy, suggesting a memorization threshold tied to training-data redundancy. Ansari (2026) showed that hallucinated citations in NeurIPS 2025 papers universally exhibit compound failures across multiple fields. 2Preprint. Under review. Cross-study comparison is hampered by methodological fragmentation: studies use differ-ent elicitation strategies, field sets (3 to 7+), and hallucination definitions (binary, threshold-based, weighted composites, categorical) (Walters & Wilder, 2023; Chelli et al., 2024; Al-jamaan et al., 2024; Szeider, 2026). No standardized benchmark existed before CiteAudit (Yuan et al., 2026), which itself checks only six fields. Mitigation approaches converge on bypassing LLM parametric memory through retrieval-augmented (Lewis et al., 2021) or tool-augmented (Nakano et al., 2022) generation. Systems such as CiteAudit, CheckIfExist (Abbonato, 2026), and BibAgent (Li et al., 2026) ground citations in authoritative databases, consistently reducing both fabrication and field-level errors. Our clibib builds on this insight by fetching authoritative BibTeX entries from the Zotero Translation Server. 

## 3 Methodology 

We evaluate BibTeX generation quality under ecologically valid conditions by prompting three frontier LLMs with natural-language paper descriptions and comparing the generated entries against multi-source ground truth. The pipeline has four stages: task formulation and prompt construction (§3.1), paper selection across four domains and three citation tiers (§3.3), ground truth construction from authoritative bibliographic databases (§3.4), and a two-stage evaluation combining deterministic field matching with LLM-as-judge adjudication (§3.6). 

3.1 Task formulation 

We adopt a known-item retrieval elicitation strategy with natural-language descriptions (KR-nat). Each prompt takes the form: 

Give me the BibTeX for {description }. Return the BibTeX entry inside <bibtex></bibtex> tags. 

where {description } is a natural-language reference to a specific paper, constructed from its metadata. For papers with three or more authors, the description follows the pattern “Title ” by First Author et al.; for two authors, “ Title ” by Author 1 and Author 2; for a single author, “ Title ” by Author. Author names use the display form from the OpenAlex record without normalization. The description deliberately omits structured metadata—DOI, year, venue, and volume—to mirror how researchers naturally refer to papers in conversation or when asking an assistant for a citation. This design occupies a specific point in the elicitation design space identified in prior work. Open-ended generation (Walters & Wilder, 2023; Chelli et al., 2024) asks models to produce reference lists from scratch, conflating retrieval failures with compositional errors. Szeider (2026) tested known-item retrieval with obfuscated citations at varying difficulty levels, but their descriptions were deliberately degraded (e.g., paraphrased titles, omitted authors) to stress-test retrieval. Our KR-nat descriptions are neither degraded nor enriched: they provide the minimal information a researcher would typically supply—title and first author—without the structured identifiers that would allow simple database lookup. The interaction is single-turn: one prompt, one response, with no follow-up or correction. We request the BibTeX entry inside XML-style <bibtex> tags to enable deterministic extraction from the response without heuristic parsing. Each of the 931 papers is queried once per model, yielding 2,793 generated BibTeX entries across three models.

3.4 Ground truth construction 

Ground truth construction was applied to all 997 initially sampled papers; the quality filtering that reduces the dataset to 931 papers is described at the end of this section. Evaluating BibTeX accuracy requires ground truth that accounts for version multiplicity: a single paper may have an arXiv preprint, conference proceedings, and journal version, each with legitimately different BibTeX representations. A generated entry citing the arXiv version of a paper that also appeared at a conference is not hallucinated—it cites a different version. Our ground truth construction pipeline discovers all citable versions of each paper, fetches authoritative BibTeX for each, and cross-validates against domain-specific databases.

Canonical field resolution. After collecting BibTeX from all sources, we resolve one canonical value per field using priority rules: DOI from OpenAlex (most authoritative); title from Zotero (best capitalization); author and venue from DBLP (CS domains) or PubMed (Medicine), falling back to Zotero; year by majority vote across sources; and volume, issue, and pages from the published version, preferring journal over proceedings over arXiv. Each canonical field records its provenance source. Of the 997 papers, 975 (97.8%) have at least one canonical field resolved; the remaining 22 papers lacked any fetchable BibTeX across all sources. Figure 5 shows the field-level coverage per domain. 

Venue synonym table. We construct a venue synonym table mapping each of the 40 venues to its known name variants—abbreviations, full names, and naming conventions observed across Zotero, DBLP, and PubMed. The table covers 141 name variants total (avg. 3.5 per venue) and is used during field-level evaluation (Section 3.6) to normalize venue name comparisons. Of the 2,793 venue evaluations, 99 (3.5%) were escalated to Stage 2 after failing synonym-table lookup. Only 5 of these were partial formatting mismatches (e.g., “BMJ: British Medical Journal” vs. the canonical “BMJ”); the remaining 94 were genuinely incorrect venues. The dominant patterns are preprint-vs-published substitutions (“arXiv preprint arXiv:1802.03268” for a paper published at ICML), confusable sibling journals within the same publisher family (“JCO Oncology Practice” vs. “Journal of Clinical Oncology”), and cross-venue substitutions within the same subfield (“Physical Review A” vs. “Physical Review Letters”). These cases reflect model errors rather than normalization gaps: the synonym table resolves formatting variation effectively, while the Stage 2 LLM judge handles the remaining semantic distinctions. 

Quality filtering. The dataset reduction from 997 to 931 papers occurred in two stages. During ground truth reconstruction, 20 papers were removed because their metadata could not be reliably resolved: year mismatches between OpenAlex and authoritative sources, misclassified entry types (e.g., conference abstracts indexed as full papers), and journal frontispieces or editorials indexed as research articles. A subsequent quality audit removed 46 additional items: proceedings volumes mistakenly included as individual papers (AI), corrections and errata (Medicine, Materials Science), Nature Physics news blurbs misclassified as quantum computing research, and off-topic papers that passed OpenAlex topic filtering in broad-scope physics journals. The 931 papers retained after filtering form the seed dataset for all subsequent generation and evaluation. All tables and figures in Section 3.3 report post-filtering counts; the version landscape and retrieval statistics above describe the pipeline as run on the initial 997 papers. 

3.5 Error taxonomy 

We consider 10 BibTeX field slots per entry: entry type , entry key , author , title , year ,

venue (journal or booktitle), volume , number , pages , and doi . Because entry key is always labeled X (citation keys are arbitrary), nine fields are evaluable per entry. Each field is independently classified into one of six labels: • C (Correct): Value matches ground truth after normalization, against any version. • M (Missing): Field absent from generated entry but present in ground truth. • F (Fabricated): Value has no verifiable source—invented content. • P (Partially correct): Meaningful overlap with ground truth but not exact (e.g., truncated author list, abbreviated venue name). • S (Substituted): Value traces to a real but incorrect source—a different paper or an incorrect version’s field. • X (Not applicable): Field not present in ground truth for this entry type (e.g., volume 

for a preprint). entry key is always X because citation keys are arbitrary. The distinction between F and S matters for diagnosis: fabricated values indicate generation from noise; substituted values indicate retrieval of the wrong record. The distinction between M and X avoids penalizing omission of fields that have no ground-truth value. 9Preprint. Under review. Not all fields apply to all entry types. For @inproceedings , volume and number are typically not applicable. For @misc entries, volume , number , and pages are typically not applicable. A field is labeled X when it is either inapplicable for the entry type or absent from all ground-truth versions. 

3.6 Evaluation pipeline 

The evaluation pipeline assigns labels to all 25,137 field-level observations (2,793 entries × 9fields) in two automated stages, with a human spot-check as a third stage. 

Stage 1: Deterministic classification. Stage 1 applies rule-based matching to assign C, M, or X labels. First, entry key fields receive X unconditionally. Next, fields inapplicable to the generated entry type and absent from all ground-truth versions receive X. Fields absent from the generated BibTeX but present in any ground-truth version receive M. For remaining fields, we apply per-field normalization before comparison: author names are compared by first-author last name (case-insensitive, diacritics stripped); titles are lowercased with LaTeX commands and braces removed; venues are resolved through the 141-variant synonym table; DOIs are lowercased with URL prefixes stripped; pages are normalized to start--end 

format; years require exact 4-digit match. A field is labeled C if its normalized value matches any ground-truth version. Unresolved fields pass to Stage 2. Stage 1 resolved 93.6% of evaluable fields (21,631 of 23,103 fields excluding X), producing only C and M labels. The resolution rate was consistent across models: Gemini 3 Flash 94.7%, Claude Sonnet 4.6 94.2%, GPT-5 92.0%. 

Stage 2: LLM-as-judge. The 1,472 fields not resolved by Stage 1 require fuzzy matching to distinguish partial matches, substitutions, and fabrications. We use Gemini 3.1 Flash Lite ( gemini-3.1-flash-lite-preview ) as the judge model via the autorubric framework 1.This model was chosen for its low cost and because it is a different model variant from the Gemini 3 Flash system under evaluation. We acknowledge the vendor overlap: the judge and one evaluated system are both Google models. Three factors mitigate bias concern: (a) Flash Lite is a distinct, smaller model variant with different training and capabilities than Flash; (b) human validation yields κ = 0.67–0.84 (Section 6.4), showing no systematic favoring of Gemini entries; and (c) Stage 2 resolves only 6.4% of evaluable fields (1,472/23,103)—93.6% receive deterministic Stage 1 labels where no LLM judgment is involved. Triangulating with an alternate-vendor judge is a natural extension for future work. Each pending field is evaluated against two binary criteria: (1) partial match —does the generated value have meaningful overlap with any ground-truth version? (2) different paper —does the value clearly come from a different work? The verdict mapping is: partial match MET → P; partial match UNMET with different paper MET → S; both UNMET → F. All 1,472 pending fields were resolved with zero failures. Appendix B reproduces the full criteria definitions, verdict mapping, and borderline examples from the human validation. 

Stage 3: Human spot-check. To validate Stage 2 labels, we draw a stratified sample of 521 fields (15% of the 3,476 Stage 2 pool) for independent human re-annotation. Stratification is by label (P/S/F) and field type (9 fields), with a minimum of 2 items per stratum, yielding 24 of 27 possible strata (3 strata have zero Stage 2 instances: no field was labeled S for 

entry type , number , or year ). The sample spans all three experimental conditions—baseline (228), single-stage clibib (175), and two-stage (118)—and is shuffled with sequential IDs to blind the annotator to condition ordering. A human annotator labels each field P, S, or F using the same two-question decision procedure as the autorubric: (1) meaningful overlap with any ground-truth value? If yes, P. (2) If no, clearly from a different paper? If yes, S; otherwise F. Raw agreement between the LLM judge and the human annotator is 80.4% (419/521); Cohen’s κ = 0.67. ## 4 Results 

We evaluate 2,793 generated BibTeX entries across 9 fields each, yielding 25,137 field-level observations. After excluding 2,034 not-applicable (X) fields, 23,103 evaluable fields remain. Overall, 83.6% of evaluable fields are correct (C), 10.1% are missing (M), 1.8% are fabricated (F), 3.5% are partially correct (P), and 1.0% are substituted (S). Approximately half of entries (50.9%) have all evaluable fields correct.

## 5 clibib as Mitigation 

The results in Section 4 establish that search-enabled models produce field-level errors across all conditions. Prior work (Section 2) suggests that grounding generation in authoritative databases can reduce such errors. We evaluate this hypothesis by providing clibib , a tool that fetches verified BibTeX from the Zotero Translation Server (which aggregates CrossRef, DBLP, PubMed, and other authoritative sources), as an additional callable tool during generation. 

5.1 System description 

clibib is a Python CLI tool and LLM agent skill that provides a unified interface to the Zotero Translation Server. 3 Given a query—a DOI, arXiv ID, ISBN, PMID, paper title, or URL—it classifies the input type and routes it to the appropriate endpoint. Identifier-based queries (DOIs, arXiv IDs) resolve deterministically through the server’s backend databases; free-text title queries trigger an ambiguous search and return the best match. URLs are normalized before lookup: arXiv PDF and HTML links are converted to abstract URLs, 

alphaxiv.org links are mapped to arxiv.org , and HuggingFace paper links are resolved to their underlying arXiv entries. The tool comprises approximately 230 lines of Python with two runtime dependencies ( requests and python-dotenv ), is released under an MIT license on PyPI, and ships as an agentskills.io -compatible skill for integration into LLM agent platforms. The property that enables hallucination mitigation is deterministic resolution : when a DOI, arXiv ID, ISBN, or PMID is provided, the Zotero Translation Server returns exactly one 

> 3https://github.com/zotero/translation-server

17 Preprint. Under review. record without any search ranking or LLM involvement. No model generates or reconstructs the metadata; the returned BibTeX reflects publisher-deposited records from CrossRef, DBLP, PubMed, and other authoritative sources. For free-text title queries, the server returns a candidate set, which clibib ranks using token-level Jaccard similarity with a substring tiebreaker. When the server returns no candidates, a CrossRef API fallback provides additional coverage. Internally, clibib routes queries through two Zotero Translation Server endpoints. URL queries (including normalized arXiv, alphaxiv, and HuggingFace links) are sent to the server’s /web endpoint, which scrapes the target page and extracts structured metadata. All other queries—DOIs, arXiv IDs, ISBNs, PMIDs, and free-text titles—are sent to the 

/search endpoint, which resolves identifiers against the server’s backend databases (Cross-Ref, DBLP, PubMed, and others). DOI-style URLs are an exception: the DOI is extracted from the URL and rerouted to /search for deterministic resolution rather than page scrap-ing. When /search returns an empty response, clibib falls back to the CrossRef API (api.crossref.org/works ), querying with the original text and retrieving up to 10 candi-dates. When either endpoint returns multiple candidates (an HTTP 300 response from Zotero, or multiple CrossRef hits), clibib ranks them by token-level Jaccard similarity between the query and each candidate’s title, with a substring-match tiebreaker, and selects the top-ranked candidate. If only one candidate is returned, it is accepted without ranking. In all cases, the resolved metadata is returned as Zotero JSON items, which clibib converts to BibTeX via the server’s /export endpoint. The output fields depend on what the upstream database deposited; clibib performs no field synthesis or inference beyond sanitizing the citation key to alphanumeric characters. This design has both strengths and limitations. Deterministic resolution, multi-identifier support, and broad coverage via the Zotero Translation Server’s aggregation of multiple databases make clibib effective for filling missing fields and replacing fabricated values. However, title-based search remains unreliable—CrossRef and Zotero frequently return incomplete or ambiguous matches—and coverage is limited to indexed databases (9.5% of lookups during ground truth construction returned not found ). The base tool provides no field-level comparison scoring and no batch mode, relying on the calling model to integrate the returned BibTeX with its own output. 

5.2 Mitigation protocol 

We evaluate two integration architectures that differ in how the model interacts with clibib :a single-stage tool loop (Algorithm 1) and a two-stage revision pipeline (Algorithm 2). 

Single-stage integration (GPT-5 and Claude). The clibib lookup function is registered alongside the native web search tool. The prompt instructs the model to: (1) search the web using the paper description to find its DOI or URL, (2) inspect the top results to identify the best match, (3) call clibib lookup with the DOI or URL, and (4) return the database result. The model drives the tool loop: it decides which query to send to clibib , interprets the response (including not found errors and title-mismatch warnings), and produces the final BibTeX. The loop runs for up to 3 tool rounds; if the model has not produced a final response after 3 rounds, a forced completion call with only web search (no clibib lookup )elicits a text response. Google’s API does not allow GoogleSearch grounding and function declarations in the same request, so Gemini is excluded from the single-stage condition. 

Two-stage integration (all models). Because the single-stage approach excludes Gemini, we also evaluate a two-stage architecture that applies uniformly to all three models (Algo-rithm 2). Phase 1 uses each model’s existing baseline output (generated with web search but without clibib ). Phase 2 performs a client-side clibib lookup using the paper’s URL, DOI, or title (in that priority order) and validates the result with a title-match gate: both titles are lowercased, tokenized to alphanumeric words, and filtered to remove single-character tokens and common stopwords; Jaccard similarity on the resulting token sets must reach 

≥ 0.3 to proceed. If the lookup succeeds and the title validates, the model receives a revision prompt (Appendix D) containing both entries and applies field-by-field reconciliation. 18 Preprint. Under review.

When titles match, the model replaces all standard bibliographic fields—authors, title, year, venue, volume, number, pages, and DOI—with the database values, retaining the baseline value only for fields the database entry lacks entirely. When titles do not match (the database returned a different paper), the model preserves the baseline entry unchanged. This asymmetry reflects the design principle that publisher-deposited metadata is authoritative for structured fields. The model’s web-search-derived entry serves as a fallback for fields outside database coverage. Ground-truth independence. If circularity were inflating the mitigation effect, the Zotero-only domain should show larger

6.3 Comparison with prior work 

Our field-level findings are broadly consistent with the cross-study pattern identified in prior work: titles are the most accurate field, DOIs the least, and numerical fields (volume, number, pages) are error-prone. Walters & Wilder (2023) reported 94–97% title accuracy; we observe 80.6%. Chelli et al. (2024) reported 16–20% DOI accuracy; we observe 75.5%, a substantial improvement attributable to search tools. Aljamaan et al. (2024) found that standalone LLMs scored at the maximum hallucination level on their weighted Reference Hallucination Score, while retrieval-augmented tools scored near zero—a pattern corroborated by the large gap between our search-enabled baselines and the non-search results reported in earlier work. Unlike these prior studies, our evaluation is version-aware: ground truth accommodates preprint-to-published metadata changes, reducing false negatives for fields such as venue, volume, and pages that shift across versions. Niimi (2025) found a log-linear correlation ( r = 0.75) between citation count and metadata accuracy, with a memorization threshold near 1,000 citations. Our citation-tier stratification provides converging evidence under search-enabled conditions: popular papers (median citations > 1 ,000) achieve the highest fully correct rates, while recent and low-citation papers degrade sharply—consistent with Niimi’s redundancy-driven account, though web search partially compensates for lower parametric familiarity. 26 Preprint. The architecture of the LLM– database interaction also matters: our ablation shows that separating retrieval from revision (two-stage) reduces regression to 0.8%, compared with 4.8% for single-pass tool loops where the model interleaves search and generation. This reinforces Szeider’s finding that how the model accesses authoritative data—not just which data source is used—determines output quality. More broadly, fully deterministic pipelines that resolve a known DOI through the Cross-Ref API to publisher-deposited metadata can achieve near-perfect field accuracy for DOI-bearing papers. However, these pipelines require a structured identifier as input, which is unavailable in the knowledge-retrieval scenario we study: users provide natural-language descriptions, not DOIs or arXiv IDs. clibib occupies a middle ground, accepting natural-language queries and resolving them to authoritative records via the Zotero Translation Server (which queries CrossRef and DBLP); a direct comparison isolating each upstream data source is future work. Boudourides (2026) frames bibliographic errors as instances of structural hallucination : sys-tematic distortion of relational architecture that remains invisible to sentence-level fluency checks. Without retrieval tools, gpt-4.1-mini achieved DOI F1 = 0.01 and omitted 91.9% of ground-truth citations; our search-enabled models achieve 75.5% DOI accuracy, confirming that retrieval substantially closes this gap. Field-level metadata errors—particularly in DOIs and venue identifiers—propagate into citation networks, corrupting link structure and distorting downstream bibliometric analyses. Our results support Boudourides’ call for pre-ingestion verification when LLM-generated bibliographic records enter scholarly infrastructure. 

6.4 Human validation of LLM judge labels 

A stratified sample of 521 fields (15% of the Stage 2 pool) was independently re-annotated by a human judge using the same two-question decision procedure as the autorubric. Overall agreement is 80.4% (419/521) with Cohen’s κ = 0.67, below the 0.70 threshold commonly used as a benchmark for substantial agreement. However, the deficit is driven by two systematic patterns rather than random noise. Table 12 shows per-field κ values. First, entry type : the LLM judge labels all type mismatches (e.g., misc vs. article or inproceedings ) as fabrications (F). By contrast, the human annotator applies the guideline that semantically related entry types constitute partial matches (P). This accounts for 37 of 39 entry type items. Second, doi : the LLM judge labels preprint-vs-published DOI pairs (e.g., an arXiv DOI vs. the published proceedings DOI for the same paper) as substitutions (S). The human annotator, by contrast, recognizes these as version variants of the same work and labels them P. This pattern accounts for 29 of 76 doi disagreements. Together, these two patterns explain 66 of the 102 total disagreements. Excluding entry type alone raises κ to 0.77; excluding both entry type and doi yields 

κ = 0.84, indicating strong agreement on the remaining seven fields. The direction of disagreement is conservative: the LLM judge over-labels F and under-labels P relative to the human annotator. Reported error rates are therefore pessimistic lower bounds on true accuracy. A sensitivity analysis replacing LLM labels with human labels on the 521-field sample shifts aggregate field-level accuracy by less than 0.2 percentage points; the disagreements have negligible impact on the paper’s conclusions. As a stronger test, restricting evaluation to the 94.1% of fields resolved deterministically by Stage 1 (exact string or normalized match, requiring no LLM judgment) yields uniformly higher accuracy but preserves all model rankings, indicating that no comparative finding in this paper depends on the LLM judge. 

## 7 Conclusion 

Search-enabled LLMs produce BibTeX entries that are better than those from base models, but still far from reliable: roughly half of the entries in our benchmark contain at least one field-level error, and deterministic verification against authoritative databases is the most effective correction strategy we evaluated. Our benchmark isolates the conditions under which errors arise (Section 4). The dominant factor is paper recency, not domain or model choice: all three frontier models degrade sharply on post-cutoff papers; search retrieval compensates for parametric gaps, but only partially. The co-occurrence analysis (Section 4.6) further reveals that errors are not uni-formly distributed across fields—identity fields (author, venue, year) tend to fail together in wholesale entry substitutions. Content fields (title, pages) fail independently. This struc-tural distinction has a practical consequence: a lightweight check of three identity fields is sufficient to catch the most damaging error mode. The mitigation evaluation (Section 5.3) demonstrates that routing LLM output through a deterministic retrieval layer produces substantial accuracy gains with minimal regression. The two-stage ablation (Section 5.4) adds a more nuanced lesson: how a tool is integrated matters, not just whether it is integrated. Separating authoritative retrieval from LLM-based revision avoids the instruction-following failures that degrade single-pass tool loops. This decoupling of retrieval from revision likely generalizes to other settings where LLMs must incorporate structured external evidence. These findings lead to a direct recommendation: LLM-generated BibTeX entries should be verified against authoritative databases before use, whether through in-context tool aug-mentation, direct database export, or post-hoc validation. Models should not be trusted to produce publication-ready bibliographic metadata from parametric memory or unstructured search results alone. Several open problems remain. Database coverage gaps limit mitigation effectiveness for recent and niche publications; expanding backend coverage and improving fallback strategies (e.g., fuzzy matching across multiple registries) would extend the reach of de-terministic approaches. Model compliance with tool-use instructions remains inconsistent, particularly under single-pass integration; understanding why models selectively ignore retrieved evidence is an open question for tool-augmented generation more broadly. Finally, standardizing BibTeX evaluation methodology—field sets, error taxonomies, and ground truth construction—would enable more direct comparison across future studies and help the community converge on shared baselines for citation quality. 28 Preprint. ## Limitations 

Several limitations constrain the generalizability of our findings. We test a single elicitation strategy (known-item retrieval with natural-language descriptions); open-ended generation or structured queries may produce different error profiles. Our benchmark covers four scientific domains; fields with different publication norms (law, humanities, social sciences) may behave differently. We evaluate three models at a single point in time; both models and search tools evolve rapidly, and our results represent a temporal snapshot. Ground truth coverage depends on OpenAlex, DBLP, PubMed, and the Zotero Translation Server; papers outside these databases cannot be fully verified, and approximately 11% of clibib 

lookups returned not found . Domain-level comparisons should be interpreted with the caveat that ground-truth provenance varies by domain: AI and Quantum Computing rely primarily on DBLP and Zotero, Medicine on PubMed, and Materials Science on Zotero alone. Because clibib and the ground truth both draw on the Zotero Translation Server, a circularity concern arises; a restricted-subset analysis on independently verified papers finds that mitigation gains are not inflated (see “Ground-truth independence” in Section 6.2). The autorubric Stage 2 evaluation introduces its own error; human validation yields κ = 0.67 overall (0.84 excluding two systematic patterns), with disagreements in the conservative direction (Section 6.4). We use vendor-recommended temperature settings (GPT-5: no temperature parameter; Claude and Gemini: T = 1.0), which introduces stochastic variation; results at T = 0 may differ for Claude and Gemini. Our primary author evaluation matches on first-author last name only; a stricter all-author criterion reduces author accuracy by 11.1 pp (Table 6), largely due to truncated co-author lists. Our evaluation covers English-language venues; extending normalization to non-Latin scripts and multilingual metadata is future work. We do not compare against purpose-built reference verification tools such as CheckIfExist or pure CrossRef export pipelines. clibib queries CrossRef and DBLP through the Zotero Translation Server, making it partially comparable, but a direct ablation isolating each data source is future work. 

## Ethics Statement 

This study evaluates commercially available LLMs on a bibliographic generation task using publicly available paper metadata. No human subjects were involved; the human validation component used annotations by the authors on model-generated outputs. All seed papers were drawn from public bibliographic databases (OpenAlex, DBLP, PubMed), and the generated BibTeX entries contain no private or sensitive information. We release our evaluation code and dataset to support reproducibility. A potential dual-use concern is that detailed error taxonomies could help bad actors generate more convincing fake references. We judge this risk to be low: the fabrication patterns we document are already widely known, and our mitigation tool ( clibib ) directly counteracts them. The net effect of this work is to make citation hallucinations easier to detect and correct, not easier to produce.

## Appendix A Extended Related Work 

A.1 Citation hallucination in LLM outputs 

Hallucination in large language models—generating fluent text unsupported by training data or grounding sources—is well documented (Ji et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024). Cita-tion hallucination is a specific instance: fabricated or corrupted references propagate into manuscripts and downstream citation analyses. When an LLM produces a bibliographic reference, two distinct failure modes arise: the model may fabricate a reference to a paper that does not exist, or it may cite a real paper with corrupted metadata (wrong authors, title, year, venue, DOI, or other fields). Both modes undermine the reliability of LLM-assisted scientific writing. Early documentation of the problem focused on fabrication rates. Alkaissi & McFarlane (2023) reported that ChatGPT generates plausible-looking but entirely fictitious references. Agrawal et al. (2024) studied hallucinated citations across domains and models. More recently, Xu et al. (2026) benchmarked 13 LLMs on citation generation, finding hallucination rates between 14% and 95% across vendors with no consistent relationship between batch size and hallucination rate. Sakai et al. (2026) identified nearly 300 published papers contain-ing at least one hallucinated citation—most published in 2025—and proposed an OCR-based detection method. Ansari (2026) analyzed 100 hallucinated citations across 53 NeurIPS 2025 papers, finding that 66% were total fabrications and 100% exhibited compound failure modes where multiple fields were simultaneously incorrect. Their taxonomy distinguishes total fabrication, partial attribute corruption, identifier hijacking, placeholder hallucination, and semantic hallucination. 

A.2 Field-level accuracy of LLM-generated bibliographic records 

Studies of field-level accuracy differ along two axes: how citations are elicited and how errors are evaluated. Three data collection approaches have emerged: (a) open-ended generation, where an LLM is prompted to produce reference lists from scratch; (b) known-item retrieval, where the LLM is given an obfuscated description of a specific paper and asked to recover the full citation; and (c) verification of existing or synthetic citations against authoritative databases. Evaluation methods diverge correspondingly—per-field error rates, weighted composite scores, categorical classification, and binary existence checks—making cross-study comparison difficult. Three studies adopt the open-ended generation approach. Walters & Wilder (2023) con-ducted the first systematic field-level study, prompting GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 to generate literature reviews across 42 multidisciplinary topics, then verifying the resulting 636 cita-tions against Google Scholar, PubMed, Scopus, WorldCat, and other databases. Fabrication rates were 55% for GPT-3.5 and 18% for GPT-4. Among non-fabricated citations, numeri-cal fields were most error-prone: volume/issue/pages were incorrect 34% of the time for GPT-3.5 and 13% for GPT-4, while dates showed 22% and 16% error rates respectively. Title accuracy was highest (94–97% correct). Chelli et al. (2024) prompted GPT-3.5, GPT-4, and Bard to generate references matching inclusion criteria for 11 systematic reviews in ortho-pedic surgery and related fields, using PubMed as ground truth. They defined a reference as hallucinated if two or more of three core fields—title, first author, year—were incorrect, a threshold that conflates partial corruption with fabrication. Title and journal accuracy exceeded 96% for non-hallucinated papers, but DOI accuracy was 16–20%. Aljamaan et al. (2024) developed a weighted Reference Hallucination Score (RHS) across 7 fields (4 major, 3 minor), with weights determined via Delphi consensus among librarians and physicians. They tested 6 systems including retrieval-augmented tools (Elicit, SciSpace); standalone LLMs scored at the maximum hallucination level, while retrieval-augmented tools scored 30 Preprint. Under review. near zero. The variation across these three studies is instructive: Walters uses post-hoc multi-database verification with binary fabricated/real judgments; Chelli thresholds on 2-of-3 core fields against PubMed; Aljamaan applies a Delphi-weighted composite score. Each captures different aspects of the same underlying problem. Szeider (2026) adopted a different elicitation strategy: known-item retrieval with obfuscated citations at varying difficulty levels. Rather than asking the model to generate references, they provided descriptions of 104 known DBLP papers and tested whether Claude Sonnet 4.5 could recover correct BibTeX entries under three configurations—web search only, DBLP-mediated, and DBLP-unmediated. Evaluation used a 6-way categorical scheme (perfect match, wrong paper, not found, incomplete metadata, incomplete author, corrupted metadata) rather than per-field error rates. Unmediated database export achieved 82.7% perfect match versus 28.2% for the web baseline. The mediated approach eliminated metadata corruption but still dropped DOI, volume, and pages 36.5% of the time because the LLM reconstructed entries from memory rather than copying verbatim. This is the only study testing architectural design choices rather than comparing models. A third group of studies evaluates citations through verification rather than generation-then-checking. Algaba et al. (2024) checked binary existence via Semantic Scholar fuzzy matching, finding that 12–36% of references generated by GPT-4, GPT-4o, and Claude 3.5 Sonnet did not correspond to any indexed paper. Their primary contribution is distribu-tional: generated references had a median citation count 1,326 higher than actual references, quantifying how LLMs amplify the Matthew effect. Chern et al. (2023) verified only three fields—title, authors, and year—via Google Scholar lookup as one component of a broader factuality framework, reporting 82.3% of LLM-generated citations as non-factual and that tool-augmented verification with GPT-4 achieved 95.24 F1 at detection. Yuan et al. (2026) introduced CiteAudit, a controlled perturbation benchmark with 9,442 citation instances where hallucinations are generated through targeted edits (keyword substitution, author perturbation, venue/year changes). Their multi-agent verification system achieved 0.903 F1 on real-world hallucinated citations, while general-purpose LLMs degraded to 0.331 F1 or below. These three studies differ fundamentally from the first group: they measure existence, factuality, or detection accuracy rather than per-field error rates across a comprehensive field set. Across these studies, title is consistently the most accurate field when the cited paper exists, and DOI is consistently the worst—often fabricated or omitted entirely. Numerical metadata (volume, pages, issue) are error-prone across all models. Two recent studies extend these findings along complementary axes. Niimi (2025) generated 100 bibliographic records across 20 computer science domains with GPT-4.1 and found a strong log-linear correlation ( r =

0.75) between citation count and metadata accuracy: papers with fewer than ∼100 citations were predominantly hallucinated, while those exceeding ∼1 ,000 citations were recalled near-verbatim, suggesting a memorization threshold tied to training-data redundancy. Boudourides (2026) introduced the concept of structural hallucination —systematic distortion of relational architecture invisible to sentence-level metrics—and evaluated gpt-4.1-mini on 50 COVID-19 publications against Dimensions.ai metadata. DOI reconstruction achieved F1 

= 0.01, and 91.9% of ground-truth citations were omitted entirely, illustrating that without retrieval tools, LLMs fail to reconstruct bibliographic structure. Methodological fragmentation further limits cross-study comparison. There is no agreed-upon minimal field set: studies evaluate 3 to 7+ fields with different inclusion criteria. Hallucination definitions range from binary fabricated/real (Walters & Wilder, 2023) to 2-of-3-field thresholds (Chelli et al., 2024) to weighted composite scores (Aljamaan et al., 2024) to 6-way categorical schemes (Szeider, 2026). Ground truth sources vary from post-hoc database search to pre-defined authoritative records to Delphi expert consensus. No standardized benchmark existed before CiteAudit (Yuan et al., 2026), and even CiteAudit checks only six fields, omitting volume and pages. Reported accuracy numbers are not directly comparable without normalizing for these differences. 31 Preprint. Under review. 

A.3 Mitigation approaches 

Retrieval-augmented generation (Lewis et al., 2021) and tool-augmented generation (Nakano et al., 2022) offer general strategies for grounding LLM outputs in external knowl-edge. Several systems have adapted these strategies specifically for citation accuracy. On the verification side, the FacTool and CiteAudit systems discussed in Section A.2 double as mitigation tools; CiteAudit’s architecture is particularly relevant here because it cascades through memory lookup, web search, and a dedicated scholar agent, providing resilience when any single source fails. CheckIfExist (Abbonato, 2026) validates bibliographic entries against CrossRef, Semantic Scholar, and OpenAlex using a multi-stage matching algorithm with cascading fallback. On the generation side, BibAgent (Li et al., 2026) constructs a benchmark of miscitation cases and uses retrieval-augmented “evidence committees” to verify claims against source documents. CiteLLM (Hong et al., 2026) generates context-aware queries and retrieves candidate papers with full-text semantic analysis, achieving 87.5% precision. CiteGuard (Choi et al., 2026) uses retrieval-augmented validation with full-text snippet retrieval to ground citation attribution. As shown in Section A.2, Szeider’s unmediated export approach eliminates metadata corruption entirely by bypassing the LLM during BibTeX construction. All of these approaches bypass LLM parametric memory for bibliographic record construc-tion. Grounding in authoritative databases, whether through retrieval augmentation, tool use, or direct export, consistently reduces both fabrication and field-level errors. Our work builds on this insight with clibib , which fetches authoritative BibTeX entries from the Zotero Translation Server to detect and correct hallucinated citations. 

## B LLM-as-judge rubric and borderline examples 

Stage 2 of the evaluation pipeline (Section 3.6) uses an LLM judge to classify the 1,472 fields that Stage 1 could not resolve deterministically. The judge evaluates each field against two binary criteria via the autorubric framework (Nair, 2026), using Gemini 3.1 Flash Lite with structured output constrained to MET /UNMET /CANNOT ASSESS per criterion. This section documents the full criteria definitions, verdict mapping, and borderline cases identified during human validation. 

B.1 Criteria definitions Criterion 1: partial match . “Does the generated value partially match any ground-truth version?” The criterion is MET if there is meaningful overlap between the generated value and at least one ground-truth value. Examples of partial matches include: a truncated or reordered author list that shares most authors with the ground truth; an abbreviated or slightly different venue name referring to the same venue (e.g., “Proc. NeurIPS” vs. “Ad-vances in Neural Information Processing Systems”); a title with minor wording differences, a missing subtitle, or formatting artifacts; a page range that overlaps or is a subset of the ground truth; a year off by one due to publication lag; or minor formatting differences in a DOI (e.g., URL prefix vs. bare identifier). The criterion is UNMET if the generated value has no meaningful overlap with any ground-truth version—the values refer to entirely different content or are fabricated with no recognizable connection to the ground truth. 

Criterion 2: different paper . “Does the generated value appear to come from a com-pletely different paper than the ground truth?” The criterion is MET if the value clearly refers to a different work. Indicators include: author names sharing no overlap with any ground-truth version; a title describing a different topic or method; a DOI resolving to a different paper; a venue/year combination inconsistent with all ground-truth versions (e.g., “ICML 2020” vs. “NeurIPS 2017”); or volume/number/pages from a different journal issue. The criterion is UNMET if the value could plausibly refer to the same paper, even if imprecise or partially wrong. When uncertain, the conservative default is UNMET —a value is flagged as different-paper only when there is clear evidence of substitution. 32 Preprint. Under review.

B.4 Borderline cases from human validation 

Human validation of 521 Stage 2 fields (Section 6.4) identified two systematic disagreement patterns between the LLM judge and the human annotator. These account for 66 of 102 total disagreements (64.7%). 

Entry-type mismatches (F vs. P). The LLM judge labeled all entry-type mismatches as fabrications (F), treating any type difference as invented content. The human annotator labeled them as partial matches (P), reasoning that semantically related BibTeX types for the same paper—e.g., misc vs. article , or misc vs. inproceedings —reflect an imprecise but not fabricated classification. This pattern accounts for 37 of the 44 F →P disagreements. For example, a generated misc entry for a paper whose ground truth is article was labeled F by the judge (no textual overlap between the type strings) but P by the human (the paper exists, and misc is a common catch-all type in BibTeX). 

Preprint-vs-published DOI versioning (S vs. P). When a generated DOI points to an arXiv preprint (e.g., 10.48550/arXiv.2510.16227 ) and the ground truth contains the pub-lished version’s DOI (e.g., 10.1162/TACL.a.611 ), the LLM judge labeled the mismatch as a substitution (S)—the structurally different registrant prefixes led it to conclude the value came from a different paper.
