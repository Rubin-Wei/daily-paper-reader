## ABSTRACT 

Existing methods for AI psychological counselors predominantly rely on supervised fine-tuning using static dialogue datasets. However, this contrasts with human experts, who continuously refine their proficiency through clinical practice and accumulated experience. To bridge this gap, we propose an Experience-Driven Lifelong Learning Agent ( PsychAgent ) for psychological counseling. First, we establish a Memory-Augmented Planning Engine tailored for longitudinal multi-session interactions, which ensures therapeutic continuity through persistent memory and strategic planning. Second, to support self-evolution, we design a Skill Evolution Engine that extracts new practice-grounded skills from historical counseling trajectories. Finally, we introduce a Reinforced Internalization Engine that integrates the evolved skills into the model via rejection fine-tuning, aiming to improve performance across diverse scenarios. Comparative analysis shows that our approach achieves higher scores than strong general LLMs (e.g., GPT-5.4, Gemini-3) and domain-specific baselines across all reported evaluation dimensions. These results suggest that lifelong learning can improve the consistency and overall quality of multi-session counseling responses.

## 1 Introduction 

Psychological counseling remains difficult to access in many settings because demand for mental-health support exceeds the supply of qualified professionals. Recent large language models (LLMs), including foundation models such as GPT-4o [ 1], Llama [ 2 ], and DeepSeek [ 3 ], have shown strong semantic understanding, making them promising for tasks ranging from empathetic support to preliminary psychological assistance. In response, the research community has increasingly specialized LLMs for mental health. Early efforts, such as ChatCounselor [ 4] and SoulChat [ 5], used domain-specific fine-tuning to improve professional capability and emotional resonance. More recent work has moved toward agent-based systems with stronger clinical structure. For example, AutoCBT [ 6] employs multi-agent collaboration to deliver structured Cognitive Behavioral Therapy, while PsyDT [ 7]introduces “Digital Twins” to model the personalized styles of specific therapists. Addressing the temporal dimension, MusPsy [ 8 ] emphasizes the importance of multi-session tracking. However, many of these systems remain largely static after training: whether relying on fixed weights or pre-defined workflows, they provide limited support for updating strategies from ongoing interactions. This static paradigm stands in stark contrast to the professional growth of human counselors. A human therapist does not become an expert solely by studying textbooks or datasets; rather, they cultivate expertise through an experience-driven loop by accumulating “clinical intuition" from years of successful and unsuccessful cases. This discrepancy suggests three key challenges in current AI counseling: 1) unlike human experts who refine techniques and abstract practical

## 2 Related Work 

2.1 LLMs for Psychological Counseling 

Recent work has adapted LLMs to mental-health support and psychological counseling through domain-specific data and supervision. Early efforts such as ChatCounselor [ 4 ], Psy-LLM [ 11 ], SoulChat [ 5], and MindChat [ 12 ] mainly improve empathy, emotional support, and counseling-oriented dialogue generation. Subsequent work injects stronger therapeutic structure into the model or system design, for example through cognitive reframing [ 13 ], CBT-grounded modeling [ 14 , 15 ], multi-agent coordination [ 6 ], or therapist-style modeling via digital twins [ 7]. In parallel, evaluation has become more systematic, covering counseling competency, therapy-specific ability, and ethical risks [ 16 , 17 , 18 ], together with broader human-evaluation frameworks for healthcare LLMs [19]. A parallel line of work emphasizes that counseling is inherently longitudinal rather than single-turn. MusPsy [ 8], PsyDial [20], and PsychEval [21] move toward multi-session data and evaluation, while TheraMind [22] incorporates cross-session planning and adaptation into the agent itself. However, these systems are still largely static after training: they may better model history or follow structured workflows, but they do not explicitly extract new therapeutic skills from accumulated interactions and internalize them into model parameters. Our work targets this gap by framing AI psychological counseling as an experience-driven lifelong learning problem. 

2.2 Experience-Driven Lifelong Learning 

The evolution of Continual Learning (CL) has undergone a paradigm shift, transitioning from merely mitigating catastrophic forgetting in static architectures to enabling dynamic adaptation in LLMs [ 23 ]. Traditional approaches largely addressed the stability-plasticity dilemma through regularization techniques like EWC [ 24 ] or experience replay mechanisms [ 25 , 26 ]. While recent frameworks have introduced sophisticated memory management [ 27 ] and reinforcement learning [ 28 ] to handle long-term interactions, they predominantly focus on data retention rather than high-level skill acquisition, often struggling with the semantic complexity of open-ended real-world tasks [29]. Building on this direction, recent work has increasingly explored Self-Evolving Agents that refine capabilities through recursive loops of experience and reflection [ 30 , 31 ]. Frameworks such as SAGE [ 32 ] and Richelieu [ 33 ] suggest that agents can improve reasoning through self-reflective critique. However, many existing systems still rely heavily on transient in-context learning or external retrieval (RAG) to exhibit “growth.” As formalized by Cai et al. ## 3 Our Methods 

As illustrated in Figure 1, we propose PsychAgent , an experience-driven framework intended to capture aspects of how counselors improve through practice. The architecture includes three components: the Memory-Augmented Planning Engine, which supports therapeutic continuity across longitudinal sessions through long-term context; the Skill Evolution Engine, which extracts successful intervention strategies into a structured hierarchical skill tree; and the Reinforced Internalization Engine, which incorporates these explicit skills into model parameters via rejection-based reinforcement. By iterating over these stages, PsychAgent is designed to reduce reliance on static imitation and heavy external retrieval. We formulate the AI psychological counseling task as a Lifelong Sequential Decision-Making problem, where an agent πθ interacts across a longitudinal timeline T = {S1, S 2, . . . , S T }. For each session St, the agent generates a response trajectory conditioned on the evolving client state Mt (Memory) and a dynamic repository of therapeutic strategies Kt (Skills). 3.1 Memory-Augmented Planning Engine 

Standard LLMs often suffer from temporal incoherence in long-context interactions, failing to maintain a consistent therapeutic trajectory. To mitigate this, the Memory-Augmented Planning Engine (Figure 1a) serves as the agent’s cognitive substrate, ensuring longitudinal continuity. This engine is designed to support longitudinal continuity through three functions: Dynamic Memory Construction , Strategic Planning , and Context-Aware Generation .

Dynamic Memory Construction. Unlike static context windows, we maintain a structured memory store Mt that evolves alongside the therapy. This is composed of two parts: • Evolving Client Profile ( Pt): Rather than a fixed intake form, the client profile is treated as a dynamic semantic memory. At the conclusion of each session St−1, the agent explicitly extracts new attributes (e.g., changes in family status, new medical diagnoses) to incrementally refine the profile: 

Pt ← Update (Pt−1, Extract attr (St−1)) (1) This helps the agent maintain an updated understanding of the client’s life circumstances. • Episodic Session Summaries ( Ht): To compress long-term history, the agent generates a structured summary ht−1

of the previous interaction, capturing key emotional shifts and intervention outcomes, which is appended to the history: Ht = Ht−1 ∪ { ht−1}.

Goal-Oriented Strategic Planning. Therapy requires foresight. Before initiating session St, the engine performs 

Plan Reasoning to synthesize the updated memory Mt = ( Pt, Ht) into a coherent roadmap. The model predicts a structured plan Gt, encompassing: 1) Therapeutic Stage: Identifying the global phase (e.g., Case Conceptualization ,

Core Intervention , Consolidation and Prevention ); 2) Session Objectives: Formulating specific, actionable goals for the current session (e.g., “Reinforce cognitive restructuring techniques"). This planning step provides a more structured framework for subsequent dialogue generation, rather than leaving the interaction purely reactive. 3.2 Skill Evolution Engine 

While the Memory Engine anchors the agent in the client’s past, the Skill Evolution Engine (Figure 1b) is designed to expand the model’s explicit repository of therapeutic techniques over time. Unlike static systems relying on fixed prompt engineering, this engine maintains a dynamic ontology of therapeutic techniques. We unify both the utilization 

and evolution of skills under a generative framework, acquired through rigorous Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) on expert-annotated cognitive tasks.

3.3 Reinforced Internalization Engine

To achieve this, we employ an iterative Rejection Fine-Tuning (RFT) mechanism [ 35 ], which mimics the human process of reflecting on and reinforcing successful counseling experiences while discarding ineffective ones.

## 4 Experimental Results 

4.1 Experimental Setups Datasets and Metrics. We build our experiments on PsychEval [ 21 ]. Following its protocol, we first perform supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on the released multi-session counseling corpus, and then reuse its pool of 2,000+ client profiles for both rollout training and evaluation. For each therapeutic school, we sample 140 client profiles, using 120 for training and 20 for evaluation. We adopt the evaluation protocol of PsychEval . Across all therapeutic schools, we report the shared counseling metrics, including PANAS, RRO, SRS, C USTOM _D IM , HTAIS, WAI, and DIALOGUE _P LANNING . In addition, each school is evaluated with two school-specific metrics: behavioral therapy with MITI and STAI, cognitive-behavioral therapy with CTRS and BDI_II, postmodernist therapy with EFT_TFS and SFBT, humanistic-existential therapy with TES and CCT, and psychodynamic therapy with PSC and IPO. Following PsychEval , we report both counselor-side and client-side results, further organized into therapy-shared and therapy-specific dimensions. 

Baselines. We compare PsychAgent against two categories of baselines to disentangle the effects of general reasoning ability and domain-specific counseling specialization. For General-purpose LLMs , we include several strong off-the-shelf LLMs: GPT-5.4 [ 9], Gemini-3 [ 10 ], Qwen3-Max [ 37 ], and DeepSeek-V3.2 [ 38 ]. These baselines represent state-of-the-art general models and allow us to assess whether strong counseling performance can be achieved from general capability alone. For Psychology-Specific LLMs , we include PsyLLM [ 11 ], PsyDTLLM [ 7], CPsyCounX [ 39 ], and TheraMind [ 22 ]. PsyLLM incorporates explicit diagnostic and therapeutic reasoning, PsyDTLLM models counselor-specific style via digital twins, CPsyCounX is fine-tuned on reconstructed counseling dialogues, and TheraMind is designed for longitudinal counseling with cross-session planning and adaptation. 

Implementation Details. We finetune PsychAgent with Qwen3-32B and set the maximum context length to 32,768 tokens. All models are trained with DeepSpeed ZeRO-3 in bf16 precision, using a per-device batch size of 1, gradient accumulation of 8, a learning rate of 1 × 10 −5, cosine decay, and 10% warmup for 3 epochs. The rollout number 

N = 8 . History masking is enabled in all experiments unless otherwise specified. Training is conducted on two servers, each equipped with 8 NVIDIA H200 GPUs.

This pattern indicates that generic memory methods, including Vanilla RAG [ 41 ], Graph RAG [ 42 ], Mem0 [ 43 ], and MemoryBank [ 44 ], may be insufficient on their own for this setting. In contrast, PsychAgent achieves higher scores across all metrics, which is consistent with the value of combining memory with skill learning and internalization. 

Case Studies To better understand why PsychAgent improves counseling performance, we examine representative cases of skill extraction and post-internalization behavior (Appendix C). Three observations are particularly notable. First, the model often distills practice-grounded skills from counseling trajectories rather than merely restating entries in the initial skill library, which is consistent with a form of self-evolution from practice (Section C.1). Second, after internalization, the model often combines existing micro-skills into new functional skills rather than only reusing predefined ones, suggesting a degree of skill-level compositional reorganization beyond the original inventory (Section C.2). Third, some internalized skill packages recur across therapeutic orientations, suggesting partial cross-context reuse of intervention patterns rather than strict therapy-specific reuse only (Section C.3). Taken together, these findings indicate broader functional reuse, though not therapy-agnostic counseling. 

## 5 Conclusions and Further Work 

We introduced PsychAgent , an experience-driven lifelong learning framework for AI psychological counseling that integrates memory-augmented planning, skill evolution, and reinforced internalization. By moving beyond static fine-tuning, PsychAgent enables the counselor agent to improve through accumulated experience and deliver more consistent, professional, and effective multi-session support. Experiments across multiple therapeutic schools show that our framework consistently outperforms both strong general-purpose LLMs and psychology-specific baselines. Future work will focus on evaluating the framework in more realistic counseling settings and strengthening safety and privacy protections for real-world deployment.

Human Evaluation Rubric and Annotation Principles 

Evaluation target Rate the counselor’s performance , rather than the client’s state, on four dimensions: Ethics ,

Interaction , Intervention , and Perception .

General principles Ratings must rely only on information explicitly stated in the dialogue; unsupported inference is disallowed. A score of 3 serves as the default when evidence is limited, performance is unremarkable, or the relevant ability is not clearly manifested. Scores of 4–5 require clear supporting evidence, and a score of 5 is used sparingly. Obvious risks, boundary violations, misreadings, and unsafe responses lower the score. Opportunity-sensitive abilities are not penalized when the relevant scenario is absent. 

Ethics Whether the counselor responds safely, respects boundaries, avoids misleading diagnosis or labeling, and does not provide clearly unsafe or inappropriate guidance. 

Interaction Whether the counselor maintains a coherent, natural, and responsive conversation, avoids obvious repetition or non sequiturs, and moves the dialogue forward at an appropriate pace and tone. 

Intervention Whether the counselor provides help that is appropriate to the client’s current problem and state, and whether the guidance is clear, feasible, and practically usable. 

Perception Whether the counselor accurately understands and responds to the client’s emotions and psychological state, conveys empathy, and helps the client better understand themselves or see possible resources, hope, or direction. 

Scoring rule 1 = clearly poor; 2 = weak; 3 = acceptable, unremarkable, or insufficient evidence; 4 = good with clear strengths and no major problems; 5 = very good, used conservatively and only when multiple clear strengths are supported by explicit evidence. 

Evidence recording For each dimension, raters provide one overall 1–5 score, one key positive evidence span, and, when applicable, one negative evidence span in a structured format. 

Why this matters. This rubric enforces evidence-grounded and conservative scoring, reduces annotation ambiguity, and makes human–LLM comparisons more interpretable and reproducible. 

The matched evaluation set contains 522 fully rated dialogues from 25 cases and 188 case-session units. This yields 6,264 dimension-level observations and 2,088 paired ratings for each rater pair. One dialogue was excluded because all three rating files failed for the same item, leaving a nearly complete matched sample. The sample is balanced across counselor models (173 PsychAgent dialogues, 175 Qwen3-Max dialogues, and 174 TheraMind dialogues), which supports comparison at the overall, by-dimension, by-model, and model-by-dimension levels.

## C Case Studies 

C.1 Extracting New Skills from Conversations 

To better understand why PsychAgent improves counseling performance, we examine whether its extracted skills merely restate the initial skill library or instead capture new practice-grounded skill units from real counseling dialogue. Here, we define a practice-grounded skill unit as an extracted skill that introduces non-trivial practical increments 

beyond the closest existing micro-skills, rather than simply rephrasing or elaborating pre-existing entries. Concretely, the comparison baseline is the v3 library within the same therapeutic orientation and stage, where micro-skills are already fine-grained and typically include when_to_use and trigger fields. As a result, a skill is counted as novel only when it adds practical structure that is not already captured by the nearest existing micro-skills. Under this criterion, the answer is clearly positive. Across 715 dialogue records, the extraction pipeline produces 9,701 atomic skills, among which 5,923 qualify as practice-grounded skill units ( B+C). This pattern holds across all five orientations: the proportion of practice-grounded skill units reaches 73.29% in BT, 69.77% in CBT, 56.0% in HET, 51.61% in PDT, and 63.49% in PMT. These results show that the dominant outcome of extraction is not restatement, but the distillation of practically enriched skill units relative to the closest existing micro-skills. The practical increments are also highly consistent across orientations. The most frequent additions are clearer operational sequencing, minimum viable actions, quantitative thresholds, structured templates, monitoring or recording 13 ICALK@ECNU 

loops, and pause or rollback rules. In other words, the model does not mainly invent completely new therapy techniques; instead, it absorbs interactional experience from counseling trajectories and re-expresses it as more executable local skill units. At the same time, our stricter global audit finds no sufficiently supported candidates for entirely novel therapy techniques relative to the initial library. Even the strongest cases are better understood as stable repackagings of existing techniques with added thresholds, templates, monitoring rules, or minimum-action designs. Representative cases from all five orientations further illustrate this pattern. 

BT: Micro-Contracts Through Thresholds and Flexible Logging 

Closest library skills Formalize and sign a behavioral contract ( 661 ); specify monitoring arrangements in the contract (662 ); implement reinforcement and punishment contingencies as specified ( 663 ). 

Representative extracted skill 

Rule-based micro-contract with thresholds and flexible logging .

Practical increment The extracted unit turns a generic contract into a concrete execution package: an explicit red-light threshold, a fallback sequence, a fixed minimum practice duration, and a logging option adapted to the client’s preferred emoji-based format. 

Dialogue evidence. Client: This week I tried audio recording, but halfway through it felt awkward, like talking to myself, so I switched to sending cat emojis to my writing buddy. After sending them, the pressure felt lower. When you suggested “just write the simplest sentence,” yesterday I only wrote “The protagonist stared at the balcony for five minutes,” and somehow ended up writing half a page. 

Counselor: “Just write the simplest sentence” often creates unexpected fluency. Let us write the rule clearly: if tension is ≥ 7/10 , first bring it down to ≤ 3/10 , and only then write for 10 minutes; for logging, use “practice/down/write” or the cat emojis you prefer. Would that work? 

Client: I think that could work. Honestly, sometimes seeing “practice/down/write” suddenly irritates me, as if I am boxed into another task. But recording with cat emojis might feel easier. 

Interpretation. This case goes beyond recovering a generic BT label such as behavioral contracting. The extracted skill adds an explicit threshold, a rollback rule, a minimum viable practice target, and a client-preferred logging format. The non-trivial increment lies in turning a broad contract into a locally executable self-management unit grounded in the client’s actual resistance and workaround. 

CBT: One-Page Process Cards with Low-Threshold Activation 

Closest library skills Attribute progress to the client to strengthen self-efficacy ( 1247 ); review what was learned and apply it in daily life ( 1433 ); emphasize that tools can transfer across situations and encourage continued use ( 1248 ). 

Representative extracted skill 

Design a one-page process card with a low activation threshold and a personalized starter cue .

Practical increment The extracted unit compresses multiple CBT elements into a reusable local workflow: step order, an optional low-burden record, a user-specific cue phrase, and concrete in-situation calibration actions. 

Dialogue evidence. Counselor: Let us organize the tools into a “one-page process card.” The skeleton is: pause when tension rises → two slow breaths → glance at one sentence on the card → calibrate one body part → return to the beat → do a before/after rating. In class, would you be willing to track “beats aligned” and “toe calibrations” as easy indicators? What sentence should we add that best fits you? 

Client: This week I tried tracking beat alignment a few times, and it worked better than just feeling anxious. But sometimes even glancing at the card makes me think, “Is recording numbers too deliberate?” What about adding, “Just get one beat right first”? I often panic on the very first beat. 

Counselor: That concern is realistic. Let us keep the record “optional + observational”: either write one fact or just record the beat count, with no performance target. “Just get one beat right first” fits your sticking point well, so let us put it on the front of the card. 

Interpretation. This case is not merely a more detailed restatement of tool use or review. The extracted skill introduces a stable mini-workflow with sequencing, low-threshold activation, and a cue phrase stated in the client’s own language. What is distilled from practice is not just “use CBT tools,” but how to make those tools deployable when the client hesitates at the point of initiation.

C.2 Operational New Functional Skills 

To better understand why internalization improves counseling performance, we compare post-internalization behavior against both the initialized skill library and the SFT baseline. Two observations are central. First, internalization does not simply increase the number of multi-skill turns. Instead, it reorganizes existing micro-skills into more stable and functionally coherent turn-level packages. Second, some of these internalized packages are reused across therapies, suggesting that the model acquires transferable counseling principles rather than only therapy-specific templates. Together, these patterns indicate that internalization pushes the model toward a more general and versatile counselor. The clearest evidence for internalization is not a mechanical increase in multi-skill turns, but the emergence of recurrent higher-order packages with clearer local intervention functions. In other words, the model does not merely place multiple skill labels in the same turn; rather, it increasingly organizes several existing micro-skills into compact intervention units that behave like new functional skills at the operational level. Importantly, these packages should not be interpreted as newly invented canonical techniques. A more conservative interpretation is that internalization compresses multiple known micro-skills into recurrent, stable, and functionally closed turn-level units. This is why the strongest evidence for compositional generalization comes from the quality and stability of these packages, rather than from raw bundle counts alone. 16 ICALK@ECNU

C.3 Cross-Therapy Reuse 

A second important effect of internalization is that some of these functional packages become reusable across therapies. Here, however, the strongest evidence does not come from exact cross-therapy bundles within the same turn, since such strict mixed-therapy combinations remain limited. Instead, the more informative pattern is cross-therapy reuse at the functional-family level : similar intervention logics recur across different therapy-specific contexts while preserving related local counseling goals. 18 ICALK@ECNU

Taken together, these findings indicate that internalization improves both the compositional organization and the portability of counseling skills. The model becomes better at turning existing micro-skills into reusable functional units, and some of these units begin to generalize across therapies.
